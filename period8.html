NATO

North Atlantic Treaty Organization, established in 1949 as a military alliance between the United States, Canada, and ten Western European nations. The alliance represented a profound shift in American foreign policy, abandoning the traditional aversion to "entangling alliances" in favor of permanent military commitments abroad. NATO's formation was driven by perceived Soviet threats to Western Europe, particularly following the communist coup in Czechoslovakia (1948) and the Berlin Blockade (1948-49). The alliance's core principle was collective defense, with Article 5 declaring that an attack on one member would be considered an attack on all. NATO led to permanent stationing of American forces in Europe, integrated military command structures, and standardized weapons and training. The Soviet Union responded by creating the Warsaw Pact (1955), formalizing the division of Europe into opposing military blocs.

Korean War

Conflict (1950-1953) beginning when North Korean forces invaded South Korea, prompting American-led United Nations intervention to prevent communist conquest of the peninsula. The war went through several phases:

  • Initial North Korean advances nearly conquering South Korea
  • UN counteroffensive under General MacArthur pushing toward Chinese border
  • Massive Chinese intervention forcing UN retreat
  • Stalemate along the 38th parallel leading to armistice in 1953

The conflict represented the first military implementation of containment policy and established patterns that would characterize later Cold War conflicts, including limited war objectives, concern about escalation to nuclear conflict, and tensions between military and civilian leadership (culminating in Truman's firing of MacArthur for insubordination). The war resulted in approximately 36,000 American deaths and massive Korean casualties, while leaving the peninsula divided along roughly the same line as before the conflict, a division that persists today.

McCarthyism

Period of intense anti-communist suspicion and investigation in the United States, named after Senator Joseph McCarthy, whose February 1950 speech claiming to have a list of communists in the State Department initiated a wave of accusations. The broader Red Scare phenomenon included:

  • House Un-American Activities Committee investigations of alleged communist influence in entertainment, education, and labor
  • Loyalty programs requiring federal employees to prove political reliability
  • Smith Act prosecutions of Communist Party leaders
  • Blacklists preventing suspected communists from employment in many industries

While genuine Soviet espionage did occur (as revealed in the Venona decrypts), McCarthy and others made reckless, unfounded accusations that damaged many innocent people's lives and careers. The excesses of McCarthyism eventually generated backlash, particularly after Edward R. Murrow's television exposé and McCarthy's self-destructive performance in the Army-McCarthy hearings (1954). The episode left lasting impacts on American politics, chilling dissent and narrowing acceptable political discourse for decades.

Sputnik (1957)

First artificial satellite, launched by the Soviet Union in October 1957, demonstrating technological capabilities that shocked the American public and policymakers. The launch created fears of a "missile gap" favoring the Soviets and anxiety about American educational and scientific competitiveness. The United States responded with initiatives including:

  • Creation of NASA to coordinate civilian space exploration
  • National Defense Education Act providing federal support for science and mathematics education
  • Accelerated missile development programs
  • Increased emphasis on science in public education

Sputnik initiated the Space Race, which became an important symbolic arena of Cold War competition, culminating in the American moon landing in 1969. The episode demonstrated how Cold War tensions could drive domestic policy changes and technological innovation while highlighting the psychological dimensions of superpower competition.

Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)

Thirteen-day confrontation between the United States and Soviet Union over the placement of nuclear-capable missiles in Cuba, bringing the world to the brink of nuclear war. The crisis began when American U-2 spy planes photographed Soviet missile installations under construction in Cuba, violating Kennedy's warning against offensive weapons on the island. Kennedy responded with a naval quarantine (blockade) of Cuba while demanding missile removal. After intense negotiations, including back-channel communications, Soviet Premier Khrushchev agreed to withdraw the missiles in exchange for American promises not to invade Cuba and (secretly) to remove Jupiter missiles from Turkey. The crisis demonstrated the dangers of nuclear brinkmanship while establishing improved communication mechanisms (including the Moscow-Washington hotline) to prevent future escalation. It also strengthened Kennedy's leadership image and temporarily improved superpower relations, leading to the Limited Test Ban Treaty (1963).

Civil Rights Movement

Brown v. Board of Education (1954)

Supreme Court decision unanimously overturning the "separate but equal" doctrine established in Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) and declaring racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional. The case combined several lawsuits organized by the NAACP Legal Defense Fund, led by Thurgood Marshall. Chief Justice Earl Warren's opinion held that separate educational facilities were "inherently unequal" and violated the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment. The ruling challenged segregation's legal foundation, though implementation faced massive resistance in the South, including "massive resistance" strategies closing public schools rather than integrating them. Subsequent decisions like Cooper v. Aaron (1958) reaffirmed federal authority to enforce desegregation, though meaningful integration proceeded slowly, accelerating only after civil rights legislation in the mid-1960s.

Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-56)

Year-long mass protest against segregated public transportation in Montgomery, Alabama, catalyzed by Rosa Parks's arrest for refusing to surrender her seat to a white passenger. The boycott was organized by the newly formed Montgomery Improvement Association, led by 26-year-old minister Martin Luther King Jr., who emerged as a national civil rights leader through his eloquent advocacy of nonviolent resistance. African Americans, who constituted 75% of bus ridership, organized carpools and walked for 381 days, creating significant economic pressure. The protest ended with the Supreme Court ruling in Browder v. Gayle that bus segregation was unconstitutional. The boycott demonstrated the effectiveness of economic pressure and nonviolent direct action, established King's leadership, and inspired subsequent protests throughout the South. It also highlighted the crucial role of women like Parks, Joann Robinson, and others in organizing and sustaining the movement.

Little Rock Nine (1957)

Crisis over the integration of Central High School in Little Rock, Arkansas, requiring federal intervention to enforce desegregation. When nine African American students attempted to attend the previously all-white school, Governor Orval Faubus mobilized the Arkansas National Guard to block their entry, directly challenging federal authority. After negotiations failed, President Eisenhower federalized the Arkansas National Guard and deployed the 101st Airborne Division to protect the students, who faced constant harassment and threats throughout the school year. The confrontation demonstrated the limits of "massive resistance" to desegregation, established federal willingness to use military force to implement court decisions, and brought the moral issues of civil rights into American living rooms through television coverage. The crisis also led to the Civil Rights Act of 1957, the first federal civil rights legislation since Reconstruction, though its provisions were relatively weak.

SCLC, SNCC, CORE

Major civil rights organizations employing different strategies and emphases:

  • Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC): Founded by Martin Luther King Jr. in 1957 following the Montgomery Bus Boycott, the SCLC coordinated activities among local black churches and leaders. It emphasized nonviolent direct action combined with King's moral leadership and religious rhetoric, organizing major campaigns in Birmingham, St. Augustine, and Selma.
  • Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC): Emerged from student sit-in movements in 1960, with initial guidance from Ella Baker. SNCC focused on grassroots organizing and direct action, including Freedom Rides, voter registration drives in the Deep South, and the Mississippi Freedom Summer. Under later leaders like Stokely Carmichael, SNCC shifted toward Black Power philosophy and separatism.
  • Congress of Racial Equality (CORE): Founded in 1942, CORE pioneered nonviolent direct action techniques, organizing the Journey of Reconciliation (1947) and the Freedom Rides (1961). Led by James Farmer during the 1960s, CORE emphasized desegregation of public accommodations and voter registration before later embracing more militant approaches under Floyd McKissick and Roy Innis.

The diversity of organizations reflected tactical and philosophical differences within the movement while enabling a multi-faceted approach to dismantling segregation.

Freedom Rides (1961)

Direct action campaign challenging segregation in interstate transportation facilities despite Supreme Court rulings declaring such segregation unconstitutional. Organized by CORE and later joined by SNCC, interracial groups of riders traveled on buses through the South, deliberately using "whites only" and "colored only" facilities at bus terminals. The riders faced brutal violence from white mobs, particularly in Anniston and Birmingham, Alabama, where buses were firebombed and riders severely beaten. When local police failed to protect riders, Attorney General Robert Kennedy dispatched federal marshals and pressured the Interstate Commerce Commission to enforce desegregation more vigorously. The Freedom Rides demonstrated the gap between legal rights and actual implementation, generated international attention through media coverage, and established a model of enduring nonviolent resistance even in the face of violence.

March on Washington (1963)

Massive demonstration for civil rights and economic justice, bringing approximately 250,000 people to the National Mall. Originally conceived by A. Philip Randolph, the march was organized by Bayard Rustin and supported by major civil rights organizations, though with tensions over goals and tactics. The demonstration featured musical performances, speeches by civil rights leaders, and culminated in Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" speech, which eloquently articulated the movement's moral vision. The march helped build momentum for the Civil Rights Act of 1964 by demonstrating broad public support for legislation, presenting a dignified image of peaceful protest, and emphasizing the movement's inclusive vision. It also represented the high point of unity among civil rights organizations before intensifying tactical and ideological divisions in later years.

Civil Rights Act (1964)

Landmark federal legislation prohibiting discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. The act's key provisions included:

  • Title II: Outlawed discrimination in public accommodations like hotels and restaurants
  • Title VI: Prohibited discrimination in federally funded programs
  • Title VII: Banned employment discrimination and created Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
  • Title VIII: Required compilation of voter registration and voting data in areas with historically low turnout

The act represented the strongest civil rights legislation since Reconstruction, facing marathon filibuster by Southern senators before passage with bipartisan support. President Johnson skillfully maneuvered the bill through Congress, invoking Kennedy's memory and using his own legislative experience. The law fundamentally altered federal government's role in civil rights enforcement and laid groundwork for subsequent legislation, though implementation faced continuing resistance and required ongoing litigation and activism.

Voting Rights Act (1965)

Federal legislation protecting voting rights, particularly in the South, where various restrictions had prevented most African Americans from voting despite the 15th Amendment. The act was prompted by violent suppression of voting rights marches in Selma, Alabama, which generated national outrage. Key provisions included:

  • Suspension of literacy tests and similar voting restrictions
  • Authorization of federal examiners to register voters in covered jurisdictions
  • Requirement that jurisdictions with history of discrimination obtain federal "preclearance" for changes to voting procedures

The legislation dramatically increased African American voter registration and political participation, leading to thousands of Black elected officials in subsequent decades. The Voting Rights Act represented a significant expansion of federal authority in state election practices and substantially fulfilled the unfulfilled promise of the 15th Amendment, though continuing efforts to restrict voting access would emerge in later decades.

Malcolm X

Influential Black nationalist leader who articulated an alternative vision to the integrationist, nonviolent approach of mainstream civil rights organizations. As spokesman for the Nation of Islam from 1952 to 1964, Malcolm X (born Malcolm Little) advocated Black separatism, self-defense against white violence, and rejection of integration as a solution to racial problems. His compelling oratory and unflinching critique of white supremacy resonated with many urban African Americans who found little hope in gradual reform. After breaking with the Nation of Islam in 1964, Malcolm expanded his perspective through international travel and founded the Organization of Afro-American Unity, advocating pan-Africanism and a more inclusive approach to building Black political power. His assassination in February 1965 cut short this evolving vision, but his ideas significantly influenced later Black Power movements, and his autobiography (published posthumously) became a foundational text in African American literature.

Great Society and Social Change

War on Poverty

Ambitious set of programs launched by President Lyndon Johnson in 1964 to address poverty in the United States, which affected approximately 20% of Americans. Key initiatives included:

  • Economic Opportunity Act: Created Job Corps, VISTA, Community Action Programs, and other initiatives administered by Office of Economic Opportunity
  • Head Start: Preschool program providing educational, nutritional, and health services to disadvantaged children
  • Medicare: Health insurance program for elderly Americans
  • Medicaid: Health insurance program for low-income Americans administered jointly with states
  • Food Stamp Program: Expanded nutrition assistance for low-income households

The programs reflected different approaches to poverty, including job training, early intervention, income support, and community empowerment. While controversial and imperfectly implemented, these initiatives significantly reduced poverty rates and created an enduring social safety net, though funding limitations (partly due to Vietnam War costs) prevented full realization of initial ambitions.

Immigration Act (1965)

Legislation that fundamentally reformed American immigration policy by abolishing the national origins quota system established in the 1920s, which had restricted immigration based on nationality with distinct preferences for Northern and Western Europeans. The act established a new system emphasizing family reunification and needed skills, with hemispheric caps rather than national quotas. While proponents downplayed the likelihood of major demographic changes, the law dramatically altered American immigration patterns, with significantly increased immigration from Asia, Africa, and Latin America. These changes contributed to growing American diversity and multicultural character in subsequent decades, though limits on Western Hemisphere immigration created new challenges, particularly regarding Mexican migration. The act represented both a response to civil rights era concerns about discrimination and Cold War interests in improving America's international image.

Environmental Movement

Growing public concern about environmental degradation, manifested in new organizations, legislation, and public consciousness during the 1960s and 1970s. Key developments included:

  • Rachel Carson's Silent Spring (1962) exposing dangers of pesticides and chemicals
  • First Earth Day (1970) involving 20 million Americans in environmental demonstrations
  • Creation of Environmental Protection Agency (1970) consolidating federal environmental functions
  • Major legislation including Clean Air Act (1970), Clean Water Act (1972), and Endangered Species Act (1973)

The movement reflected shifting priorities in American society, with greater emphasis on quality of life issues rather than material growth alone. It built on earlier conservation traditions while focusing more explicitly on pollution, public health, and environmental justice. Though achieving significant policy reforms, the movement faced ongoing tension with economic interests and disagreements about appropriate balances between environmental protection and development.

Counterculture

Youth-centered cultural rebellion against mainstream American values and institutions during the 1960s and early 1970s. The counterculture encompassed diverse elements including:

  • Rejection of conventional social norms regarding sexuality, dress, and behavior
  • Exploration of alternative spirituality, often drawing from Eastern religions
  • Communal living experiments challenging nuclear family structures
  • Drug experimentation, particularly with marijuana and psychedelics
  • New musical forms expressing countercultural values and perspectives

Though affecting only a minority of young people directly, countercultural influences gradually penetrated mainstream society through music, fashion, language, and attitudes. The movement reflected prosperity that allowed younger generations to prioritize self-expression and personal fulfillment rather than economic security, while also responding to perceived hypocrisy in mainstream institutions. By the mid-1970s, the cohesive counterculture had largely dissolved, though its cultural influences remained significant.

Women's Liberation Movement

Resurgent feminist activism emerged in the 1960s, building on earlier women's rights traditions while developing new analyses, organizations, and tactics. The movement included multiple strands:

  • Liberal feminism: Exemplified by the National Organization for Women (founded 1966), focused on legal equality, workplace discrimination, and political representation
  • Radical feminism: Emerging from New Left and civil rights activism, emphasized patriarchy as a fundamental social system requiring comprehensive transformation
  • Cultural feminism: Focused on building women's culture, institutions, and solidarity apart from male-dominated society

Despite diverse approaches, feminists achieved significant changes including Title IX educational protections, expanded reproductive rights (culminating in Roe v. Wade, 1973), increased workplace opportunities, legal reforms regarding domestic violence and sexual assault, and transformed cultural attitudes about gender roles. The movement faced both external backlash and internal tensions regarding race, class, sexuality, and strategy, but fundamentally altered American gender relations and institutions.

Stonewall Riots (1969)

Series of spontaneous protests against police harassment at the Stonewall Inn, a gay bar in New York City's Greenwich Village, marking a pivotal moment in the LGBT rights movement. When police raided the bar on June 28, 1969, patrons and neighborhood residents resisted arrest and confronted officers, with demonstrations continuing for several days. The riots catalyzed more militant activism, transforming earlier "homophile" organizations focusing on respectability and education into a more assertive gay liberation movement demanding full equality and visibility. Within months, organizations like the Gay Liberation Front and Gay Activists Alliance formed, and the first gay pride marches commemorating Stonewall occurred in several cities in June 1970. The events represented a crucial transition point in LGBT organizing, though full legal equality would require decades of continued activism and cultural change.

American Indian Movement

Pan-tribal activism emerging in the late 1960s to address Native American civil rights, sovereignty, and cultural preservation. Founded in Minneapolis in 1968 to address urban Native issues like police brutality, AIM evolved to include reservation communities and broader indigenous concerns. The movement employed direct action tactics including:

  • Occupation of Alcatraz Island (1969-1971) highlighting broken treaties and land rights
  • Trail of Broken Treaties caravan to Washington D.C. (1972) presenting a 20-point program for reform
  • Occupation of Wounded Knee on Pine Ridge Reservation (1973) protesting tribal government corruption and federal policies

AIM blended civil rights advocacy with cultural revitalization and indigenous sovereignty claims, facing significant federal opposition including FBI counterintelligence operations. While achieving limited immediate policy changes, the movement increased public awareness of Native issues, strengthened tribal identity, and contributed to subsequent developments including the Indian Self-Determination Act (1975) and growing tribal assertion of sovereign rights in later decades.

Vietnam War and Impact

Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964)

Congressional authorization for military action in Vietnam, passed with near-unanimous support following reported North Vietnamese attacks on American destroyers in the Gulf of Tonkin. The resolution gave President Johnson authority to take "all necessary measures" to repel attacks and prevent further aggression, effectively allowing presidential discretion to expand American involvement without formal declaration of war. The precipitating incidents were misrepresented to Congress, with the second alleged attack likely never occurring. Johnson later used the resolution to justify massive escalation, including sustained bombing campaigns and deployment of over 500,000 American troops. The resolution's passage represented congressional abdication of war powers and established patterns of presidential military action without formal congressional authorization that would persist in later conflicts. Growing awareness of the circumstances surrounding its passage contributed to subsequent skepticism about government claims regarding the war.

Tet Offensive (1968)

Massive coordinated attack by North Vietnamese and Viet Cong forces throughout South Vietnam during the Vietnamese New Year (Tet) holiday in January 1968. Though American and South Vietnamese forces eventually repelled the attacks and inflicted heavy casualties on communist forces, the offensive contradicted official optimism about the war's progress and demonstrated the enemy's continuing strength and determination. Media coverage, particularly of fighting in Saigon and the ancient capital of Hue, shocked the American public and intensified skepticism about military claims. The offensive's psychological impact contributed to Johnson's decision not to seek reelection and to begin peace negotiations, while widening the "credibility gap" between government statements and public perceptions. Militarily unsuccessful, the offensive nonetheless represented a strategic turning point by undermining American political will to continue the conflict indefinitely.

My Lai Massacre

Mass killing of unarmed Vietnamese civilians by American soldiers in March 1968, revealed publicly in November 1969 through investigative reporting by Seymour Hersh. Under Lieutenant William Calley's command, soldiers from Charlie Company killed approximately 500 civilians, predominantly women, children, and elderly persons, in the village of My Lai. The massacre was initially covered up by military authorities, with only Calley ultimately convicted (though later pardoned by President Nixon). The revelations intensified anti-war sentiment and raised profound questions about American conduct, military training, and leadership in Vietnam. The incident exemplified broader patterns of civilian casualties and ethical compromises in a war where distinguishing combatants from non-combatants proved difficult, while highlighting tensions between military discipline, wartime psychology, and moral responsibility.

Pentagon Papers

Classified Department of Defense study documenting American decision-making in Vietnam from 1945 to 1967, leaked to the New York Times in 1971 by former defense analyst Daniel Ellsberg. The 7,000-page study revealed that multiple administrations had misled the public about U.S. involvement, expanding the war while expressing doubts about its winability. The Nixon administration obtained a temporary injunction against publication, leading to a landmark Supreme Court case (New York Times Co. v. United States) upholding press freedom to publish the documents. The papers demonstrated the gap between public justifications and private assessments of the war, intensifying public skepticism about government truthfulness. The episode represented a crucial confrontation between executive power and press freedom, with lasting implications for governmental secrecy and journalistic responsibility in national security reporting.

Anti-War Movement

Diverse coalition opposing American involvement in Vietnam, evolving from small protests in the early 1960s to a mass movement by decade's end. The movement included multiple constituencies with varying approaches:

  • Student activists: Organizations like Students for a Democratic Society organized campus protests, teach-ins, and draft resistance
  • Religious peace advocates: Clergy and laity providing moral opposition and practical assistance to conscientious objectors
  • Veterans: Returning soldiers forming Vietnam Veterans Against the War to share firsthand experiences
  • Mainstream political figures: Senators like J. William Fulbright and Eugene McCarthy challenging war policies from within institutions

While never representing a majority of Americans, the movement effectively used media attention, electoral politics, and mass demonstrations (including the 1969 Moratorium involving millions) to increase pressure for deescalation and withdrawal. Though often portrayed negatively in media and by politicians, the movement contributed significantly to shifting public opinion and constraining policy options, demonstrating civil society's capacity to influence foreign policy.

Paris Peace Accords (1973)

Agreement ending direct American military involvement in Vietnam after years of negotiations. The accords provided for:

  • Ceasefire between North and South Vietnamese forces
  • Withdrawal of remaining American troops
  • Return of American prisoners of war
  • Recognition of the existing division between North and South Vietnam
  • Establishment of a Council of National Reconciliation to work toward political solution

Neither side fully adhered to the agreement's terms, with fighting continuing between Vietnamese forces. For Americans, the accords provided "peace with honor" allowing military disengagement without admitting defeat, though Nixon's promises to maintain South Vietnamese independence through continued aid and potential reintervention were undermined by Watergate and congressional restrictions. The agreement reflected America's declining ability and willingness to sustain military involvement, while effectively postponing rather than resolving the fundamental conflict over Vietnamese unification.

Fall of Saigon (1975)

Capture of South Vietnam's capital by North Vietnamese forces in April 1975, culminating the communist victory in the Vietnam War. Following American withdrawal and congressional reduction of military aid, South Vietnamese forces collapsed rapidly against a massive North Vietnamese offensive. The fall was marked by chaotic evacuation of remaining Americans and some South Vietnamese allies, with dramatic helicopter rescues from the U.S. embassy. The event represented the definitive failure of American efforts to prevent communist unification of Vietnam despite massive military investment. The war's outcomes included:

  • Over 58,000 American deaths and 150,000 wounded
  • Several million Vietnamese, Cambodian, and Laotian deaths
  • Lasting environmental damage from defoliants and unexploded ordnance
  • Deep American political and cultural divisions
  • Enduring caution about military interventions ("Vietnam Syndrome")

Though ostensibly fought to prevent communist expansion, the war's aftermath saw relatively limited strategic consequences for American interests in Southeast Asia, raising profound questions about the conflict's necessity and conduct.

Political Realignment

Watergate Scandal

Political scandal stemming from the Nixon administration's illegal activities and subsequent cover-up, leading to the only presidential resignation in American history. The scandal began with the June 1972 break-in at Democratic National Committee headquarters by operatives connected to Nixon's reelection campaign, but expanded to encompass broader abuses of power including:

  • White House "plumbers" unit conducting illegal activities against perceived enemies
  • Campaign finance violations and political sabotage
  • Misuse of federal agencies including FBI, IRS, and CIA for political purposes
  • Systematic obstruction of justice coordinated from the White House

The scandal unfolded through investigative journalism (particularly by Washington Post reporters Woodward and Bernstein), congressional hearings, and legal proceedings, with crucial evidence from White House tapes recording Nixon's involvement in the cover-up. Facing certain impeachment and conviction, Nixon resigned in August 1974. The scandal prompted reforms including campaign finance regulations, intelligence oversight, and the Independent Counsel statute, while significantly diminishing public trust in government institutions and executive authority.

Oil Crisis (1973)

Economic shock following the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) oil embargo against the United States and other nations supporting Israel in the Yom Kippur War. The embargo quadrupled oil prices, created fuel shortages symbolized by long gas lines, and contributed to stagflation—the previously rare combination of high inflation and economic stagnation. The crisis exposed American economic vulnerability to foreign resource suppliers and the limitations of post-World War II growth assumptions based on cheap energy. Policy responses included conservation measures (55 mph speed limit, daylight saving time adjustments), creation of the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, and initiatives to develop alternative energy sources. The crisis accelerated structural economic changes already underway, contributed to the decline of American manufacturing dependent on cheap energy, and influenced foreign policy calculations regarding Middle Eastern stability and energy security.

Stagflation

Unusual economic condition combining high inflation and high unemployment that characterized the 1970s, contradicting traditional economic models suggesting these phenomena should move in opposite directions. Multiple factors contributed to stagflation:

  • Oil price shocks increasing production costs across the economy
  • Vietnam War spending and Great Society programs expanding money supply
  • End of Bretton Woods system and dollar devaluation
  • Declining productivity growth and international competitive pressures

Traditional Keynesian policies proved ineffective, as stimulating demand worsened inflation while anti-inflation measures increased unemployment. The Federal Reserve under Paul Volcker ultimately broke the inflation cycle through tight monetary policy causing a severe recession in the early 1980s. Stagflation profoundly affected American politics and policy, discrediting Keynesian approaches, contributing to Carter's defeat, and facilitating the rise of supply-side economics and deregulation under Reagan. The experience fundamentally altered economic assumptions that had guided postwar prosperity, shifting focus toward price stability and market-oriented policies.

Rise of New Right

Conservative political movement emerging in the 1960s and 1970s, reacting against perceived excesses of liberalism, counterculture, and government expansion. The movement encompassed several interconnected elements:

  • Economic conservatism: Opposition to taxation, regulation, and government spending
  • Social conservatism: Defense of traditional family structures and religious values against secularization
  • National security conservatism: Advocacy for military strength and assertive anti-communism
  • Grassroots mobilization: New organizations like the Moral Majority engaging previously apolitical constituencies

The movement built intellectual foundations through think tanks like the Heritage Foundation, media outlets like National Review, and academics like Milton Friedman, while developing electoral strength through figures like Barry Goldwater, George Wallace, and ultimately Ronald Reagan. Though representing diverse and sometimes contradictory positions, the New Right effectively channeled discontent with social change, economic difficulties, and perceived American decline into a coherent political force that would reshape American politics in subsequent decades.

Camp David Accords (1978)

Peace agreement between Egypt and Israel brokered by President Jimmy Carter, representing the first peace treaty between Israel and an Arab state. Following intensive negotiations at the presidential retreat at Camp David, Egyptian President Anwar Sadat and Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin signed the framework for peace, with a formal treaty concluded in 1979. Key provisions included:

  • Israeli withdrawal from the Sinai Peninsula captured in 1967
  • Egyptian recognition of Israel's right to exist
  • Establishment of diplomatic relations between the two countries
  • Framework for addressing Palestinian autonomy (though with limited implementation)

The agreements significantly reduced the likelihood of major Arab-Israeli war and established a pattern for potential future peace agreements based on land-for-peace formulas. However, they did not resolve broader Arab-Israeli conflicts or Palestinian issues, and Sadat's assassination by Egyptian militants opposed to the treaty demonstrated the internal risks of pursuing peace. For Carter, the accords represented a major diplomatic achievement, though the limited success of his other foreign policy initiatives and domestic economic problems ultimately overshadowed this accomplishment.

Iran Hostage Crisis (1979-81)

International crisis beginning when Iranian students seized the American embassy in Tehran, taking 52 American diplomats and citizens hostage for 444 days. The crisis stemmed from:

  • Historical American support for the Shah, including CIA involvement in overthrowing democratically elected government in 1953
  • American granting of the deposed Shah admission to the U.S. for medical treatment
  • Revolutionary Iranian regime under Ayatollah Khomeini seeking to consolidate power through anti-American actions

The crisis dominated Carter's final year in office, with nightly news coverage, failed rescue attempt (Operation Eagle Claw), and frozen diplomatic relations. The hostages were released minutes after Reagan's inauguration, following negotiations mediated by Algeria. The episode demonstrated American vulnerability to asymmetric challenges, caused lasting rupture in U.S.-Iranian relations, and contributed significantly to Carter's election defeat. The crisis reflected broader tensions between traditional American support for strategic allies versus concern for human rights and democratic values, a dilemma that would persist in subsequent Middle Eastern policy.

Key Terms to Know

  • Containment Policy: Strategy aimed at preventing the spread of communism
  • Marshall Plan: Economic aid program for Western European recovery
  • Détente: Period of reduced tensions between U.S. and Soviet Union in 1970s
  • Jim Crow: System of racial segregation and discrimination in the South
  • Brown v. Board of Education: Supreme Court case declaring school segregation unconstitutional
  • Great Society: Johnson's domestic program addressing poverty and inequality
  • Title IX: Legislation prohibiting sex discrimination in education
  • Domino Theory: Belief that communism in one country would spread to neighbors
  • Silent Majority: Nixon's term for Americans supporting traditional values
  • Détente: Nixon-era reduction in Cold War tensions through diplomacy

Period 8: 1945–1980 – Cold War and Social Movements

Period 7 Period 9

Key Themes

  • Geopolitical tensions between U.S. and USSR.
  • Civil Rights and other social justice movements.
  • Cultural shifts and political realignments.

Early Cold War

Origins of the Cold War

Geopolitical conflict between the United States and Soviet Union emerging after World War II. The tensions originated from:

  • Ideological differences between American capitalism/democracy and Soviet communism
  • Disagreements over the postwar status of Eastern Europe
  • Mutual suspicion and misinterpretation of the other's actions
  • Vacuum of power in Europe and Asia following the defeat of Germany and Japan
  • Development of atomic weapons, raising stakes of international conflict

The conflict was formalized in speeches like Churchill's "Iron Curtain" address (1946) and the Truman Doctrine (1947), which established the framework of containment that would guide American foreign policy for decades. Unlike previous conflicts, the Cold War was primarily waged through economic pressure, propaganda, espionage, and proxy wars rather than direct military confrontation between the superpowers.

Iron Curtain

Term popularized by Winston Churchill in a 1946 speech at Westminster College in Missouri to describe the division of Europe between Western democracies and Soviet-dominated communist states. Churchill declared that "an iron curtain has descended across the Continent" as Soviet forces consolidated control over Eastern European countries. The metaphor vividly captured the physical and ideological barrier that separated the Eastern and Western blocs during the Cold War, with restricted movement, communication, and information flow across this divide. The Iron Curtain was ultimately manifested in physical barriers like the Berlin Wall (1961) and would remain in place until the revolutions of 1989.

Containment Policy

Foundational American strategy for confronting Soviet power, articulated by diplomat George F. Kennan in his "Long Telegram" (1946) and "X Article" (1947). The policy aimed to prevent further expansion of Soviet influence through economic, diplomatic, and military counter-pressure without direct military confrontation. This approach accepted existing Soviet control in Eastern Europe while drawing a line against further expansion, particularly in Western Europe, the Mediterranean, and East Asia. Containment was implemented through diverse programs and initiatives, including:

  • Truman Doctrine: Aid to Greece and Turkey to resist communist pressure
  • Marshall Plan: Economic assistance for Western European recovery
  • NATO: Military alliance binding Western democracies
  • Korean War: Military intervention to prevent communist takeover of South Korea

The policy evolved over time, with debates about its geographic scope and appropriate methods, but remained the central framework of American Cold War strategy until the 1980s.

NATO

North Atlantic Treaty Organization, established in 1949 as a military alliance between the United States, Canada, and ten Western European nations. The alliance represented a profound shift in American foreign
