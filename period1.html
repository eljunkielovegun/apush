Period 1: 1491–1607 – Pre-Columbian to Early Contact

Period 9 Period 2

Key Themes

  • Diverse Native American societies adapted to their environments.
  • European exploration driven by the 3 G's: Gold, Glory, and God.
  • Columbian Exchange transformed global economies and ecologies.

Native American Societies (Pre-Contact)

Northeast Woodlands

Iroquois Confederacy (Haudenosaunee) developed a sophisticated political system with the Great Law of Peace. They practiced agriculture with the "Three Sisters" (corn, beans, squash), which allowed for efficient farming and nutrition.

Southeast

Mississippi culture created large mound-building settlements. Cherokee, Creek, and Seminole established complex agricultural societies with hierarchical social structures and extensive trade networks.

Great Plains

Nomadic tribes like Sioux, Comanche, and Blackfoot followed buffalo herds for survival. They developed horse culture after European contact, which dramatically changed their hunting and warfare capabilities.

Southwest

Pueblo peoples built multi-story adobe dwellings in areas with limited water. Hopi and Zuni created extensive irrigation systems that allowed for farming in arid environments.

Northwest Coast

Tlingit, Haida, and Chinook developed resource-rich societies with potlatch ceremonies and totem poles. These cultures had abundant food supplies from fishing and developed complex art forms.

Early European Exploration

Christopher Columbus (1492)

First voyage led to permanent European awareness of the Americas. Though he was searching for a route to Asia, his "discovery" initiated an era of exploration and colonization.

Spanish Conquistadors

Hernán Cortés conquered Aztec Empire (1519-1521) with a small force aided by local allies and disease. Francisco Pizarro defeated Inca Empire (1532) using similar tactics and exploiting internal divisions.

Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)

Divided newly discovered lands between Spain and Portugal. This papal-sanctioned agreement gave Spain rights to most of the Americas while Portugal received Brazil and claims in Africa and Asia.

Bartolomé de las Casas

Spanish priest who advocated for humane treatment of indigenous peoples. His writings documented Spanish abuses and influenced later colonial policies toward Native Americans.

The Columbian Exchange

Crops to Old World

Corn, potatoes, tomatoes, chocolate, tobacco, squash, beans, and peppers were introduced to Europe and Africa. These crops transformed diets and agricultural practices worldwide.

Crops to New World

Wheat, sugar, coffee, rice, bananas, and citrus fruits were brought to the Americas. Livestock including horses, cattle, pigs, sheep, and chickens also transformed the American landscape.

Disease Impact

Smallpox, measles, influenza, and other diseases devastated Native populations who lacked immunity. Up to 90% population decline occurred in some regions, fundamentally altering the balance of power.

Economic Transformation

New crops and resources changed global trade patterns and diets. The influx of precious metals from the Americas contributed to inflation in Europe but also financed further exploration and colonization.

Spanish Colonization

Encomienda System

Spanish colonizers were granted control over indigenous populations for labor. This system led to widespread abuse and contributed to population decline among Native peoples.

Mission System

Religious institutions established to convert indigenous peoples to Christianity. While providing some protection, missions also disrupted traditional ways of life and exposed Native Americans to European diseases.

Silver Mining

Mines at Potosí (Bolivia) and Zacatecas (Mexico) became crucial to the Spanish Empire. The silver extracted funded Spanish power in Europe but cost countless indigenous lives through forced labor.

Social Hierarchies

Complex racial classification system developed in Spanish colonies. Peninsulares (Spanish-born) were at the top, followed by criollos (Spanish descent born in Americas), mestizos (mixed), and indigenous and enslaved African peoples at the bottom.

Key Terms to Know

  • Three Sisters: Agricultural system of corn, beans, and squash used by many Native American societies
  • Matrilineal Society: Social organization where descent is traced through the female line, common in many Native American tribes
  • Reconquista: Spanish reconquest of Iberian Peninsula from Muslim control, influencing later colonial attitudes
  • Conquistadors: Spanish explorers and soldiers who led expeditions to conquer territories in the Americas
  • Mestizo: Person of mixed European and indigenous ancestry
  • Columbian Exchange: Transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and technologies between hemispheres
  • Epidemic Disease: Widespread infectious diseases like smallpox that devastated Native populations
  • Encomienda: Spanish labor system granting colonizers control over Native labor
  • Mercantilism: Economic theory promoting exports and accumulation of precious metals
  • Joint-Stock Company: Business enterprise where investors share risk and profit
