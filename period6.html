Period 6: 1865–1898 – The Gilded Age

Period 5 Period 7

Key Themes

  • Rapid industrialization and urbanization.
  • Rise of big business and labor movements.
  • Immigration and nativist responses.

Industrialization

Transcontinental Railroad (1869)

The completion of the first railroad spanning the continent, connecting the eastern rail network with California. The Central Pacific Railroad built eastward from Sacramento while the Union Pacific Railroad built westward from Council Bluffs, Iowa, meeting at Promontory Summit, Utah. The project, heavily subsidized by federal land grants and bonds, dramatically reduced travel time and shipping costs across the country. It facilitated westward expansion, resource extraction, and national market integration while displacing Native American populations and requiring massive immigrant labor, particularly Chinese workers on the Central Pacific.

Technological Innovation

The post-Civil War era saw an explosion of inventions and technological developments that transformed daily life and industrial production. Key innovations included:

  • Thomas Edison's electric light bulb (1879) and subsequent development of electrical infrastructure
  • Alexander Graham Bell's telephone (1876)
  • Christopher Sholes's practical typewriter (1867)
  • The Bessemer process for mass-producing steel, enabling skyscrapers and larger bridges
  • Petroleum refining techniques pioneered by John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil

These technologies enabled mass production, improved communication, and created entirely new industries, dramatically accelerating economic growth while disrupting traditional occupations and ways of life.

Rise of Big Business

The era saw the emergence of massive industrial corporations with unprecedented economic power. Business leaders developed new organizational forms, including:

  • Horizontal integration: Controlling multiple companies in the same stage of production (exemplified by Rockefeller's Standard Oil)
  • Vertical integration: Controlling multiple stages of production and distribution (exemplified by Carnegie Steel)
  • Trusts and holding companies: Legal structures that centralized control over previously competing firms

By 1900, the largest corporations controlled vast portions of their industries, with market shares often exceeding 70-80%. This concentration of economic power raised concerns about political influence, unfair competition, and worker exploitation.

Andrew Carnegie & John D. Rockefeller

Andrew Carnegie rose from Scottish immigrant origins to dominate the American steel industry. His Carnegie Steel Company pioneered vertical integration, controlling everything from raw materials to finished products. Carnegie ruthlessly suppressed labor organization and cut costs but also espoused the "Gospel of Wealth," arguing that the rich had an obligation to use their fortunes for social improvement. He ultimately sold his company to J.P. Morgan (forming U.S. Steel) and devoted his later life to philanthropy, funding libraries, universities, and cultural institutions.

John D. Rockefeller built Standard Oil into the world's largest oil refining company through horizontal integration, eventually controlling 90% of American oil refining. His business tactics included secret rebates from railroads, predatory pricing, and covert ownership of supposedly independent companies. Like Carnegie, Rockefeller was both ruthless in business and generous in philanthropy, establishing foundations that funded medical research, education, and public health initiatives. The Supreme Court's 1911 order to break up Standard Oil as a monopoly came after his retirement.

Finance Capitalism

The late 19th century saw the rise of powerful financial institutions that increasingly controlled industrial corporations. J.P. Morgan exemplified the investment banker who orchestrated industrial consolidation, most famously creating U.S. Steel in 1901 by merging Carnegie Steel with other companies to form the world's first billion-dollar corporation. Morgan and other bankers served on multiple corporate boards, creating interlocking directorates that centralized economic decision-making. This concentration of financial power came to a head in the Panic of 1893, when Morgan personally organized a bailout of the federal government, highlighting the influence of private bankers over the national economy.

Labor Movements

Working Conditions

Industrial workers faced harsh conditions including:

  • Long hours (typically 10-12 hours daily, six days per week)
  • Low wages, often insufficient for basic needs
  • Dangerous working environments with high accident rates
  • Child labor, particularly in textile mills and mines
  • Few legal protections and limited government regulation

Workers had little job security and could be fired without notice or cause. Economic downturns like the depressions of 1873 and 1893 brought widespread unemployment and wage cuts. These conditions, combined with growing awareness of the vast wealth accumulated by industrialists, fueled labor organizing and industrial conflict.

Knights of Labor

Founded in 1869, the Knights of Labor was the first major national labor organization in the United States. Under Terence Powderly's leadership (1879-1893), the Knights organized both skilled and unskilled workers regardless of race, gender, or ethnicity—a radical position for the time. The organization advocated broader social reforms, including an eight-hour workday, equal pay for equal work, abolition of child labor, and worker cooperatives as alternatives to wage labor. After initial successes, including the Southwest Railroad Strike of 1886, the Knights declined following the Haymarket Riot, internal conflicts, and competition from craft-based unions.

American Federation of Labor

Founded in 1886 under Samuel Gompers, the AFL focused on skilled craft workers organized by trade rather than industry. Unlike the Knights of Labor, the AFL pursued "bread and butter" issues like wages, hours, and working conditions rather than broad social transformation. Gompers advocated "pure and simple unionism," using collective bargaining and strikes to secure immediate improvements for members. The AFL's exclusionary practices often left out unskilled workers, immigrants, women, and racial minorities, but its pragmatic approach proved more durable than earlier labor organizations, establishing unions as permanent institutions in American economic life.

Labor Strikes

The era saw numerous major labor conflicts, including:

  • Great Railroad Strike (1877): Spontaneous nationwide strike against wage cuts that required federal troops to suppress, resulting in approximately 100 deaths
  • Haymarket Riot (1886): Labor rally in Chicago where a bomb killed several police officers, leading to controversial trials and executions of anarchist leaders despite lack of evidence
  • Homestead Strike (1892): Violent conflict at Carnegie Steel plant in Pennsylvania, where Pinkerton detectives and strikers engaged in armed battle before state militia secured the plant for strikebreakers
  • Pullman Strike (1894): Nationwide railroad strike beginning at the Pullman Palace Car Company near Chicago, eventually broken by federal troops under President Cleveland

These conflicts highlighted deep divisions in American society and the willingness of government to intervene on behalf of employers. The failure of major strikes, often due to government intervention, limited organized labor's growth during this period.

Immigration and Urbanization

New Immigrants

Between 1880 and 1920, more than 20 million immigrants arrived in the United States, with the source of immigration shifting from Northern and Western Europe to Southern and Eastern Europe. These "new immigrants" included Italians, Greeks, Poles, Russians, Hungarians, Jews, and others who differed from earlier immigrants in language, religion, and customs. They typically settled in urban industrial centers, providing crucial labor for factories while transforming American culture. Despite their economic contributions, these immigrants faced discrimination, with critics claiming they could not assimilate into American society and represented threats to American jobs and political institutions.

Urban Growth

The period saw explosive urban growth, with the percentage of Americans living in cities more than doubling between 1860 and 1900. Cities expanded both outward and upward, with new transportation technologies (streetcars, elevated trains, subways) enabling suburban development while steel-frame construction and elevators allowed for taller buildings. Urban growth outpaced infrastructure development, creating severe problems with sanitation, water supply, housing, and transportation. Cities became increasingly segregated by class and ethnicity, with immigrants clustering in densely populated ethnic enclaves while the wealthy retreated to exclusive neighborhoods or suburbs.

Tenements

Urban working-class families, particularly immigrants, typically lived in overcrowded tenement buildings with inadequate light, ventilation, and sanitation. New York's "dumbbell tenements," designed to maximize profit for landlords, packed multiple families into small apartments, with many residents sharing a single toilet and water source. These conditions contributed to high rates of disease, infant mortality, and fire deaths. Jacob Riis's influential book and photography collection "How the Other Half Lives" (1890) exposed these conditions to middle-class audiences, generating support for housing reforms like the New York Tenement House Law of 1901, which mandated improved ventilation, fire safety, and sanitation.

Political Machines

Urban political organizations that dominated many city governments by exchanging votes for services and favors. The most famous, New York's Tammany Hall under "Boss" William Tweed, controlled city contracts, patronage jobs, and municipal services. Machines provided essential assistance to immigrants and the poor—jobs, food in hard times, legal help, social services—in exchange for political loyalty. While often corrupt and inefficient, these organizations filled gaps in public services that government was not yet prepared to address. Machines faced opposition from middle-class reformers advocating civil service reform, efficiency, and professional government, setting up an enduring tension in urban politics.

Settlement House Movement

Progressive reform movement that established community centers in poor urban neighborhoods to address immigrant needs and promote assimilation. Jane Addams founded Hull House in Chicago in 1889, providing educational classes, childcare, cultural programs, and advocacy for better housing and working conditions. Settlement houses were typically staffed by educated middle-class women who lived in the communities they served, bridging class and ethnic divides. The movement expanded understanding of poverty's social causes, pioneered social work as a profession, and advanced reforms in areas like child labor, public health, and women's rights while promoting cultural pluralism rather than forced Americanization.

The West

Settlement Patterns

Western settlement accelerated after the Civil War, with population growth driven by diverse groups including:

  • Homesteaders seeking land under the Homestead Act of 1862
  • Miners pursuing gold, silver, and other mineral strikes
  • Cattle ranchers establishing operations on the Great Plains
  • Railroad workers, particularly Chinese laborers in the West
  • Religious minorities like Mormons seeking refuge from persecution

Settlement patterns varied by region, influenced by climate, available resources, and transportation access. Aridity limited traditional farming in much of the West, requiring irrigation projects and new agricultural techniques. Western development was heavily dependent on federal policies, including land grants, military protection, and infrastructure investment.

Homestead Act (1862)

Federal legislation offering 160 acres of public land to settlers who would live on and improve the land for five years. The act allowed for relatively democratic distribution of land, enabling people of modest means to become landowners. However, its effectiveness was limited by several factors: 160 acres was often insufficient in arid western regions; many settlers lacked capital for necessary equipment and improvements; speculators and railroads acquired substantial landholdings; and the most desirable lands were often claimed before ordinary settlers arrived. Still, the act facilitated the settlement of approximately 270 million acres and created opportunities for women and some minorities to acquire land independently.

Cattle Kingdom

Brief but iconic period of open-range cattle ranching on the Great Plains from approximately 1866 to 1887. Following the Civil War, Texas ranchers drove millions of longhorn cattle north to railheads in Kansas, where they were shipped to eastern markets. The industry thrived on free access to public grazing land, the expansion of railroads, and strong eastern demand for beef. Cowboys, including significant numbers of Black and Mexican vaqueros, developed specialized skills for this work. The open range system declined due to overgrazing, harsh winters in 1886-87, falling beef prices, and conflicts with farmers over land use. It was replaced by more intensive ranch operations on fenced private land.

Native American Conflicts

Western settlement led to intensified conflicts with Native American nations who resisted displacement from their lands. Major conflicts included:

  • Great Sioux War (1876-77): Sparked by gold discovery in the Black Hills, culminating in the Battle of Little Bighorn where Lakota and Cheyenne warriors defeated Lt. Col. George Custer's forces
  • Nez Perce War (1877): Conflict arising from the tribe's refusal to move to a reservation, featuring Chief Joseph's strategic 1,500-mile retreat attempt
  • Apache Wars (1861-1886): Extended conflict ending with Geronimo's surrender as the last major Native resistance in the Southwest

These conflicts ended with the defeat of Native resistance and confinement to reservations, often on the poorest lands. Military campaigns were supplemented by the destruction of buffalo herds, which undermined the economic foundation of Plains Indian cultures.

Ghost Dance Movement

Native American spiritual revival that spread across the Plains in 1889-90. The movement, initiated by Paiute prophet Wovoka, prophesied the restoration of Native lands, return of buffalo herds, and reunion with deceased ancestors through ritual dance and peaceful coexistence. Different tribes adapted the teachings to their own traditions, with some Lakota interpretations incorporating the belief that "ghost shirts" would protect wearers from bullets. Government agents, misinterpreting the movement as preparation for uprising, attempted to suppress it on the Lakota reservations. This suppression culminated in the Wounded Knee Massacre (1890), where U.S. cavalry killed approximately 250 Lakota men, women, and children, effectively ending organized Native resistance.

Dawes Act (1887)

Federal policy that attempted to assimilate Native Americans by dividing tribal lands into individual allotments. The act granted 160 acres to each family head and smaller parcels to individuals, with "surplus" land sold to white settlers. Citizenship was offered to allotment recipients who "adopted the habits of civilized life." While advocates claimed the policy would protect Native land rights and promote assimilation, its effects were devastating: tribal landholdings declined from 138 million acres to 48 million by 1934; communal governance structures were undermined; and many individual allotments were lost through fraud, tax sales, or economic necessity. The policy reflected the era's assimilationist ideology that indigenous cultures must give way to "civilization."

Politics of the Gilded Age

Political Corruption

The era was characterized by widespread corruption at all levels of government. At the federal level, scandals included:

  • Credit Mobilier Scandal: Scheme where Union Pacific Railroad executives created a construction company to overcharge for building the railroad, bribing congressmen to prevent investigation
  • Whiskey Ring: Conspiracy involving distillers and treasury officials to defraud the government of liquor taxes
  • Teapot Dome Scandal: Sale of naval oil reserves to private companies in exchange for bribes

State and local corruption was equally pervasive, with legislators routinely accepting bribes from railroads and other businesses seeking favorable treatment. This environment of corruption fueled growing calls for reform from both farmers and urban progressives.

Civil Service Reform

Movement to replace the patronage or "spoils" system with merit-based government employment. The assassination of President Garfield in 1881 by a disappointed office-seeker galvanized support for reform, leading to the Pendleton Civil Service Act of 1883. The act established competitive examinations for many federal positions and prohibited political assessments on government employees. Initially covering only about 10% of federal jobs, civil service gradually expanded to include most government positions. The reform weakened political machines by limiting patronage while creating a more professional bureaucracy, though critics noted that standardized exams often favored educated middle-class candidates over working-class and immigrant aspirants.

Populist Movement

Political movement representing primarily southern and western farmers facing economic hardship due to falling crop prices, high railroad rates, and tight credit. The movement began with organizations like the Grange and Farmers' Alliances before culminating in the People's Party (Populists), which advocated:

  • Inflation through free silver or paper money to ease debt burdens
  • Government ownership of railroads and telegraph lines
  • A graduated income tax
  • Direct election of senators
  • The subtreasury plan to provide agricultural credit

The Populists showed initial strength in the 1892 election but declined after their fusion with Democrats around William Jennings Bryan in 1896. Though politically unsuccessful, many Populist reforms were later adopted during the Progressive Era.

Social Darwinism

Intellectual movement applying Charles Darwin's biological concepts to human society, arguing that economic competition naturally selected the "fittest" individuals for success. Popularized by Herbert Spencer and William Graham Sumner, Social Darwinism suggested that poverty resulted from personal deficiencies rather than systemic factors and that government assistance interfered with natural selection. This perspective provided ideological justification for laissez-faire economics, minimal government regulation, and extreme wealth inequality. Social Darwinism influenced immigration restriction, eugenics movements, and imperialism while facing opposition from religious leaders, reform-minded social scientists, and labor activists who emphasized social factors in economic outcomes.

Key Terms to Know

  • Gilded Age: Term coined by Mark Twain describing the era's gaudy wealth concealing social problems
  • Robber Barons: Derogatory term for industrial leaders who amassed wealth through exploitative practices
  • Captains of Industry: Positive term for business leaders who drove economic growth and progress
  • Vertical Integration: Business strategy controlling multiple stages of production
  • Horizontal Integration: Business strategy controlling multiple companies in the same industry
  • Gospel of Wealth: Andrew Carnegie's philosophy that the rich should use their wealth for social good
  • Social Gospel Movement: Religious movement emphasizing Christian principles to address social problems
  • New Immigration: Shift from Northern/Western European to Southern/Eastern European immigrants
  • Americanization: Process of assimilating immigrants to American culture and values
  • Dawes Severalty Act: Law dividing tribal lands into individual allotments to assimilate Native Americans
