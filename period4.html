Period 4: 1800–1848 – Expansion and Reform

Period 3 Period 5

Key Themes

  • Democratization of politics and rise of the "common man."
  • Market Revolution transforming the economy.
  • Social reform movements gaining momentum.

Jeffersonian Era

Election of 1800

Often called the "Revolution of 1800," this election marked the first peaceful transfer of power between opposing parties in the United States. Thomas Jefferson's Democratic-Republican Party defeated John Adams's Federalists after a bitter, divisive campaign. The election initially resulted in an Electoral College tie between Jefferson and his running mate Aaron Burr, requiring the House of Representatives to decide the outcome. The crisis led to the 12th Amendment, which modified the Electoral College process.

Marbury v. Madison (1803)

Landmark Supreme Court case that established the principle of judicial review—the Court's power to declare laws unconstitutional. Chief Justice John Marshall, while denying William Marbury's petition for his commission as justice of the peace, declared that a provision of the Judiciary Act of 1789 was unconstitutional. This decision greatly enhanced the power of the judiciary as a check on the other branches and established the Supreme Court as the final interpreter of the Constitution.

Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804-06)

Commissioned by President Jefferson to explore the Louisiana Purchase territory and find a water route to the Pacific. Led by Meriwether Lewis and William Clark, the Corps of Discovery traveled over 8,000 miles, documented hundreds of plants and animals unknown to Western science, established relations with Native American tribes, and gathered crucial information about the geography of the West. Native American guide Sacagawea was instrumental to the expedition's success, helping with navigation, translation, and securing horses from the Shoshone.

Embargo Act (1807)

Jefferson's attempt to avoid war through economic pressure by prohibiting American ships from trading with foreign ports. The embargo devastated American shipping and exports while failing to significantly impact Britain or France, demonstrating the young nation's limited economic leverage. The unpopular measure led to widespread smuggling and was replaced with the Non-Intercourse Act in 1809, which restricted trade only with Britain and France.

War of 1812

Sometimes called the "Second War of Independence," this conflict with Britain arose from maritime disputes, British support of Native American resistance, and American expansionist ambitions toward Canada. Key events included the burning of Washington D.C., the successful defense of Baltimore (inspiring "The Star-Spangled Banner"), and Andrew Jackson's victory at New Orleans (ironically fought after peace was negotiated). The Treaty of Ghent (1814) largely restored pre-war conditions, but the conflict stimulated American manufacturing and fostered a stronger sense of nationalism.

Jacksonian Democracy

Election of 1828

Andrew Jackson's victory over John Quincy Adams represented the ascendance of a new democratic ethos in American politics. Jackson, the first president not from the social elite, portrayed himself as a champion of the common man against corrupt aristocrats. The election saw unprecedented voter participation following the expansion of white male suffrage across many states. It also marked the evolution of the Democratic Party and the demise of the National Republican Party, which would eventually be replaced by the Whigs.

Nullification Crisis (1832-33)

Constitutional confrontation over South Carolina's attempt to nullify federal tariffs viewed as favoring northern industry at southern expense. Led by John C. Calhoun, South Carolina claimed states could declare federal laws void within their borders. President Jackson forcefully rejected nullification as unconstitutional and secured passage of the Force Bill authorizing military action. The crisis was defused by a compromise tariff, but it established precedents for states' rights arguments that would culminate in secession debates before the Civil War.

Bank War

Jackson's campaign against the Second Bank of the United States, which he viewed as an unconstitutional monopoly that benefited the wealthy at the expense of ordinary citizens. When Bank president Nicholas Biddle sought early recharter in 1832 as a campaign issue, Jackson vetoed the bill with a powerful populist message. Jackson subsequently removed federal deposits from the Bank, essentially crippling it. The Bank's demise contributed to a less regulated banking environment and eventual economic instability.

Trail of Tears (1838)

Forced relocation of southeastern tribes to territories west of the Mississippi River, particularly the Cherokee, Creek, Chickasaw, Choctaw, and Seminole nations. Though the Supreme Court in Worcester v. Georgia (1832) ruled that states could not extend laws over Native American lands, Jackson refused to enforce the decision. The subsequent removal policy, formalized in the Indian Removal Act of 1830, led to the deaths of thousands during the journey, particularly among the Cherokee, whose forced march in harsh winter conditions became known as the "Trail of Tears."

Spoils System

Practice of rewarding political supporters with government positions, expanded under Jackson with the principle that rotation in office was democratic and that government jobs required no special expertise. While criticized as fostering corruption and incompetence, defenders argued it made government more responsive to the people and prevented the formation of an entrenched bureaucracy. The system persisted until the late 19th century, when civil service reform was implemented.

Reform Movements

Abolitionists

Advocates for the immediate end of slavery, as opposed to gradual abolition or colonization plans. William Lloyd Garrison's newspaper The Liberator (founded 1831) demanded immediate emancipation without compensation to slave owners. Frederick Douglass, an escaped slave, became a powerful orator and writer whose autobiography and newspaper North Star advanced the cause. Female abolitionists like Sojourner Truth and the Grimké sisters connected antislavery activism with women's rights. The abolitionist movement faced violent opposition in both North and South, highlighting the deep divisions slavery created in American society.

Women's Rights

Growing movement to improve women's legal status and expand their rights in society. Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott organized the Seneca Falls Convention (1848), which produced the Declaration of Sentiments modeled on the Declaration of Independence, proclaiming "all men and women are created equal" and listing women's grievances. Though primarily focused on suffrage, the movement also addressed issues like property rights, education access, and employment opportunities. Many women's rights advocates gained experience in other reform movements, particularly abolition.

Education Reform

Efforts to establish universal public education and improve teaching methods. Horace Mann, as secretary of the Massachusetts Board of Education, advocated for free, universal public education as essential for democracy and social mobility. His reforms included standardized curricula, longer school years, and improved teacher training. Education reform was linked to other social movements, with advocates arguing that an educated populace was necessary for addressing social problems and maintaining republican government.

Prison Reform

Movement to improve conditions and emphasize rehabilitation rather than punishment. Dorothea Dix championed humane treatment of the mentally ill, documenting abysmal conditions in jails and almshouses where many mentally ill people were confined. Her advocacy led to the establishment of state-funded asylums designed as therapeutic environments. Other reformers developed competing models of prison management, including the Auburn system (congregate work with enforced silence) and the Pennsylvania system (complete isolation intended to foster reflection).

Transcendentalism

Philosophical and literary movement emphasizing individualism, intuition, and the divinity in nature. Led by Ralph Waldo Emerson, whose essays "Nature" and "Self-Reliance" articulated core transcendentalist ideas, and Henry David Thoreau, whose experiment in simple living at Walden Pond and essay "Civil Disobedience" influenced later social movements. Transcendentalists critiqued materialism and conformity in American society, advocating for authentic self-expression and spiritual connection with nature.

Utopian Communities

Experimental societies established to create ideal social arrangements outside mainstream society. Notable communities included:

  • Shakers: Religious sect practicing celibacy, communal property, and gender equality
  • Brook Farm: Transcendentalist experiment combining intellectual pursuits with agricultural labor
  • Oneida Community: Founded by John Humphrey Noyes, practiced "complex marriage" and selective reproduction
  • New Harmony: Robert Owen's attempt to create a cooperative community based on rational education and scientific principles

Though most utopian communities were short-lived, they served as laboratories for social innovation, testing ideas that would later influence mainstream reforms.

Economic Transformation

Market Revolution

Fundamental shift from subsistence to market-oriented production that transformed the American economy between 1815 and 1850. Farmers increasingly specialized in cash crops for distant markets rather than diverse crops for local consumption. This transition was enabled by improved transportation, technological innovation, and new financial institutions. The market revolution altered work patterns, social relationships, and living arrangements, creating new economic opportunities but also increasing dependence on market fluctuations.

Transportation Revolution

Dramatic improvements in transportation infrastructure that facilitated economic growth and westward expansion. Key developments included:

  • Erie Canal (1825): Connected the Great Lakes to the Hudson River and New York City, drastically reducing shipping costs and time
  • Cumberland Road (National Road): First federally funded highway, extending from Maryland to Illinois
  • Railroads: Beginning in the 1830s, railroads expanded rapidly, surpassing canals in importance by the 1850s
  • Steamboats: Revolutionized river travel, particularly on the Mississippi River system

These transportation improvements facilitated regional economic specialization, accelerated westward settlement, and strengthened ties between eastern cities and western territories.

Industrial Development

Growth of manufacturing, particularly in the Northeast. The Lowell Mills in Massachusetts exemplified early American industrialization, employing young women in textile production under the "Lowell system," which attempted to provide a respectable, controlled environment for female workers. Other important developments included the spread of the factory system, increasing mechanization of production, and early labor organization in response to harsh working conditions. While still behind Britain industrially, the United States was establishing the foundation for later industrial expansion.

Cotton and Slavery

The cotton gin, invented by Eli Whitney in 1793, dramatically increased cotton processing efficiency, making short-staple cotton cultivation profitable across the South. This technological innovation had profound social consequences, revitalizing slavery when it might otherwise have declined economically. Cotton production expanded westward into newly acquired territories, increasing demand for slave labor. By 1850, cotton represented over half of American exports, linking the northern textile industry, southern plantations, and international markets in a complex economic relationship.

Territorial Expansion

Louisiana Purchase (1803)

Acquisition of approximately 828,000 square miles from France for $15 million, doubling the size of the United States. President Jefferson, despite constitutional reservations about executive power, seized the opportunity when Napoleon offered to sell the entire territory rather than just New Orleans as initially sought. The purchase secured control of the Mississippi River, provided vast lands for westward expansion, and established a precedent for territorial growth through purchase rather than conquest.

Monroe Doctrine (1823)

Foreign policy statement warning European powers against further colonization or intervention in the Western Hemisphere. While the United States lacked the military power to enforce this doctrine independently, it aligned with British interests in Latin American independence from Spain, giving it practical effect. The doctrine became a cornerstone of American foreign policy, later used to justify U.S. intervention in Latin America.

Missouri Compromise (1820)

Legislative agreement that temporarily resolved the issue of slavery in western territories by admitting Missouri as a slave state and Maine as a free state, while prohibiting slavery in the remainder of the Louisiana Purchase territory north of the 36°30' parallel. The compromise maintained the balance between slave and free states in the Senate but established the precedent of congressional regulation of slavery in the territories, a principle that would be contested in later decades.

Texas Annexation

Incorporation of the Republic of Texas into the United States in 1845 after nine years as an independent nation. Texas had declared independence from Mexico following conflicts over Mexican centralization and American settlement. The annexation was controversial because it enlarged slave territory and risked war with Mexico, which never recognized Texan independence. President Tyler pushed the annexation through just before leaving office, and the boundary dispute with Mexico subsequently led to the Mexican-American War.

Oregon Territory

Region in the Pacific Northwest jointly occupied by the United States and Britain under an 1818 agreement. American interest increased with missionary activity and settler migration along the Oregon Trail in the 1840s. The "Fifty-four Forty or Fight" slogan reflected expansionist demands for the entire region up to Russian Alaska. The dispute was resolved peacefully in 1846 with a treaty establishing the boundary at the 49th parallel, giving the United States the present-day states of Oregon, Washington, and Idaho.

Key Terms to Know

  • Louisiana Purchase: Acquisition that doubled U.S. territory; promoted westward expansion
  • Manifest Destiny: Belief that American expansion across the continent was divinely ordained
  • Market Revolution: Shift from subsistence to market-oriented production
  • Industrial Revolution: Transition from hand production to machine manufacturing
  • Declaration of Sentiments: Document from Seneca Falls Convention outlining women's rights goals
  • The Liberator: William Lloyd Garrison's influential abolitionist newspaper
  • Temperance Movement: Campaign against alcohol consumption
  • Second Great Awakening: Religious revival fueling abolitionist and temperance movements
  • Lowell System: Model of textile production employing young women in supervised factory settings
  • Kitchen Cabinet: Jackson's informal advisors, outside his official cabinet
