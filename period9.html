Period 9: 1980–Present – Contemporary America

Period 8 Period 1

Key Themes

  • Rise of conservatism and neoliberal policies.
  • Technological advancements and globalization.
  • Ongoing debates over social and economic issues.

Conservative Resurgence

Reagan Revolution

Fundamental political and ideological shift represented by Ronald Reagan's presidency (1981-1989), which transformed American governance and policy. Key elements of the Reagan presidency included:

  • Tax cuts through the Economic Recovery Tax Act (1981) reducing top marginal rates from 70% to 28%
  • Deregulation of industries including transportation, telecommunications, and finance
  • Confrontational approach to the Soviet Union and increased defense spending
  • Reduction in federal domestic programs and devolution of authority to states
  • Appointment of conservative judges reshaping judiciary for decades

Reagan's leadership style emphasized optimistic rhetoric about American exceptionalism and potential, contrasting with perceptions of malaise and decline during the Carter administration. While personally popular across party lines, Reagan's policies generated significant controversy, particularly regarding their impact on economic inequality, environmental protection, and vulnerable populations. Nevertheless, his approach fundamentally altered the terms of political debate, with even Democratic successors accepting aspects of his policy framework.

Reaganomics

Economic policies under the Reagan administration, also known as supply-side economics or "trickle-down economics" by critics. The approach emphasized:

  • Tax reduction: Particularly on higher incomes and businesses, to stimulate investment and entrepreneurship
  • Deregulation: Removing government restrictions on business operations to increase efficiency
  • Reduced government spending: Particularly on social welfare programs
  • Anti-inflation monetary policy: Supporting Federal Reserve Chair Paul Volcker's tight money policies to control inflation

The policies contributed to ending stagflation and initiated a period of economic growth, though with significant disparities in who benefited. Critics noted growing inequality, with median wages stagnating while top incomes rose dramatically. The approach also led to record federal deficits despite rhetoric about fiscal responsibility, as tax cuts and defense increases outpaced spending reductions in other areas. The economic philosophy underlying Reaganomics represented a significant departure from post-New Deal consensus about government's economic role and continued to influence policy debates decades later.

Religious Right

Political movement mobilizing evangelical and fundamentalist Christians into electoral politics, particularly within the Republican Party. Previously less politically engaged, these communities became increasingly active in response to:

  • Supreme Court decisions like Engel v. Vitale (school prayer) and Roe v. Wade (abortion)
  • Perceived threats to traditional family structures and values
  • Changes in sexual mores and gender roles
  • Concerns about secular humanism in education and media

Organizations like the Moral Majority (founded by Jerry Falwell in 1979) and Christian Coalition (led by Pat Robertson and Ralph Reed) built effective political operations focusing on voter registration, candidate support, and issue advocacy. The movement significantly influenced Republican positions on social issues like abortion, gay rights, and school prayer, while providing crucial electoral support for conservative candidates. Religious conservatives represented an important component of the Republican coalition, though sometimes experiencing tension with libertarian and business-oriented conservatives who prioritized economic issues over social conservatism.

Supreme Court Shifts

Transformation of the federal judiciary through conservative appointments, particularly to the Supreme Court. Reagan's appointments of Sandra Day O'Connor (first female justice), Antonin Scalia, and Anthony Kennedy, followed by George H.W. Bush's appointment of Clarence Thomas, shifted the Court's ideological balance. These changes produced significant jurisprudential developments:

  • More restrictive interpretation of civil rights and affirmative action (Richmond v. Croson, Adarand v. Peña)
  • Greater deference to states in federalism disputes (United States v. Lopez)
  • Narrower interpretation of abortion rights while maintaining Roe's core holding (Planned Parenthood v. Casey)
  • Expanded interpretation of Second Amendment individual rights (District of Columbia v. Heller under Roberts Court)

The judiciary's transformation reflected conservatives' long-term strategy to shape constitutional interpretation through judicial appointments, recognizing courts' power to affect policy beyond legislative cycles. While the Court remained ideologically mixed through this period with some liberal victories, the conservative legal movement substantially altered constitutional doctrine in ways that outlasted individual administrations.

End of Cold War and Foreign Policy

End of Cold War (1991)

Dissolution of the Soviet Union and end of the decades-long geopolitical and ideological conflict that had structured international relations since World War II. The Cold War's conclusion resulted from multiple factors:

  • Soviet economic stagnation unable to sustain military competition
  • Reform efforts under Mikhail Gorbachev (glasnost and perestroika) that unintentionally weakened central control
  • Nationalist movements within Soviet republics seeking independence
  • Reagan administration's military buildup and assertive diplomacy
  • Democratic revolutions in Eastern Europe, symbolized by the Berlin Wall's fall in 1989

The Soviet Union formally dissolved on December 25, 1991, replaced by Russia and 14 other independent republics. This transformation represented an extraordinary victory for American Cold War strategy and dramatically reconfigured global politics. The United States emerged as the world's sole superpower, with unprecedented military, economic, and cultural influence, though new security challenges would soon emerge to replace the relatively stable Cold War framework.

New World Order

President George H.W. Bush's term for post-Cold War international relations, envisioning increased cooperation through international institutions and American leadership. This concept informed the administration's approach to several international crises:

  • Panama: 1989 military intervention removing dictator Manuel Noriega
  • German Reunification: Diplomatic support for integrating East Germany into West German democracy and NATO
  • Gulf War (1991): U.S.-led coalition under UN authorization expelling Iraqi forces from Kuwait
  • Somalia: Humanitarian intervention to address famine and civil war (continuing into Clinton administration)

The approach reflected optimism about American capacity to shape a more peaceful, democratic world order following communism's collapse. However, nationalist conflicts in Yugoslavia and elsewhere, humanitarian crises exceeding international capacity to respond, and emerging challenges like terrorism and weapons proliferation revealed limitations to this vision. While avoiding the triumphalism some advocated, the Bush administration sought to institutionalize American preeminence through multilateral cooperation rather than unilateral action—a balance subsequent administrations would approach differently.

Gulf War

1991 military conflict following Iraq's invasion and occupation of Kuwait, representing the first major post-Cold War test of international cooperation against aggression. Led by the United States, a broad coalition including Arab states responded with:

  • UN Security Council resolutions authorizing force if Iraq didn't withdraw
  • Operation Desert Shield deploying forces to protect Saudi Arabia
  • Operation Desert Storm combining massive air campaign with ground offensive

The war ended with decisive coalition victory, Kuwait's liberation, and significant destruction of Iraqi military capability, though Saddam Hussein remained in power. The conflict demonstrated American conventional military superiority in the post-Cold War environment while establishing precedents for multinational operations under international authorization. However, unresolved issues including Iraq's internal repression, weapons programs, and regional ambitions would lead to continuing confrontation and ultimately a second Iraq War in 2003 under very different international circumstances.

9/11 Attacks (2001)

Terrorist attacks on September 11, 2001, when Al-Qaeda operatives hijacked four commercial aircraft, crashing two into the World Trade Center towers in New York City, one into the Pentagon, and one (diverted by passengers) into a Pennsylvania field. The attacks killed nearly 3,000 people, destroyed the iconic towers, and caused the first suspension of all air travel in U.S. history. The immediate aftermath included:

  • Surge of national unity and patriotism across partisan lines
  • Declaration of "War on Terror" by President George W. Bush
  • Military operation against Afghanistan's Taliban regime harboring Al-Qaeda
  • Creation of Department of Homeland Security and enhanced security measures
  • Passage of USA PATRIOT Act expanding surveillance authorities

The attacks transformed American foreign policy, shifting focus from great power competition to counterterrorism and leading to the longest wars in American history in Afghanistan and Iraq. Domestically, they altered the balance between security and civil liberties while generating ongoing debates about appropriate responses to terrorism, treatment of Muslim Americans, and America's role in the Middle East.

War on Terror

Multifaceted campaign against terrorist organizations and state sponsors following the 9/11 attacks, encompassing military, intelligence, diplomatic, and legal dimensions. Major components included:

  • Afghanistan War (2001-2021): Initially focused on removing Taliban regime and disrupting Al-Qaeda, evolving into nation-building effort
  • Iraq War (2003-2011): Controversial invasion justified by claims about weapons of mass destruction and terrorism links
  • Enhanced interrogation techniques: Disputed as potential torture under international law
  • Detention facilities: Including Guantánamo Bay holding "enemy combatants" outside normal legal frameworks
  • Expanded surveillance: Both internationally and domestically, raising privacy concerns

The campaign produced mixed results, with Al-Qaeda leadership largely decimated but new terrorist organizations emerging, particularly the Islamic State (ISIS). The wars in Afghanistan and Iraq became America's longest military engagements, costing trillions of dollars and thousands of American lives while producing questionable strategic benefits. The experience prompted reconsideration of counterterrorism approaches and America's capacity to reshape other societies, influencing subsequent administrations' greater caution about military interventions.

Economic and Social Changes

Technological Revolution

Transformation of society through digital technologies, particularly personal computing, the internet, and mobile devices. Key developments included:

  • Personal computers: From hobbyist products to ubiquitous tools in homes and workplaces
  • Internet: Evolving from specialized academic network to global information system
  • World Wide Web: User-friendly interface making internet accessible to general public
  • Mobile technology: Smartphones and tablets combining computing, communication, and media
  • Social media: New platforms for connection, content sharing, and community formation

These technologies revolutionized business models, cultural production, political organization, and personal relationships. Economic impacts included productivity gains, new industries, shifting employment patterns, and growing concerns about automation replacing jobs. Social impacts encompassed everything from changing friendship networks to transformed privacy expectations and information consumption patterns. The revolution continued to accelerate, with emerging technologies like artificial intelligence and virtual reality promising further transformations.

Globalization

Intensification of worldwide economic, political, and cultural integration through increased trade, investment, migration, and information flows. The process accelerated in the 1990s through developments including:

  • Trade agreements: NAFTA, World Trade Organization establishment
  • Financial deregulation: Increased cross-border capital flows
  • Container shipping: Standardized global supply chains
  • Digital communication: Enabled coordination across distances and timezones
  • Post-Cold War opening: Incorporated former communist countries into global economy

Globalization generated both benefits and dislocations in American society. It created efficiency gains and consumer benefits through lower prices and product diversity, while enabling economic development in many previously poor countries. However, it also contributed to manufacturing job losses in traditional industrial regions, wage pressure on less-skilled workers, and cultural anxieties about national identity. These tensions eventually generated populist backlash visible in movements opposing trade agreements and immigration in the 2010s.

Immigration Patterns

Significant demographic change through immigration, with foreign-born population growing from approximately 5% in 1970 to 13-14% by the 2020s. Key patterns included:

  • Source regions: Predominantly Latin America (especially Mexico) and Asia, unlike earlier European-dominated immigration
  • Settlement patterns: Traditional gateway cities but also new destinations in Southeast, Midwest, and rural areas
  • Educational diversity: Both high-skilled professionals and less-educated workers seeking economic opportunity
  • Legal status: Mix of permanent residents, temporary visa holders, refugees, and unauthorized immigrants

Immigration generated complex economic impacts, contributing to growth, entrepreneurship, and innovation while creating distributive effects varying by region and economic sector. Politically, immigration became increasingly contentious, with debates about border security, pathways to legal status for unauthorized immigrants, and refugee policies. Major reform efforts repeatedly failed in Congress despite bipartisan attempts, leaving policies misaligned with economic and demographic realities. Immigration nonetheless continued reshaping American society through cultural influences, demographic trends, and political realignments.

Increasing Polarization

Growing division between political parties in ideology, policy preferences, and partisan identity, accompanied by declining cooperation across party lines. The polarization manifested in several dimensions:

  • Congressional voting: Declining bipartisan legislation and cooperation
  • Geographic sorting: Increasing concentration of partisans in distinct communities
  • Media consumption: Fragmented information environment with separate partisan ecosystems
  • Social identity: Strengthening partisan identity and negative feelings toward opposing party

Multiple factors contributed to this trend, including party realignment following civil rights legislation, media changes reducing shared information sources, primary election systems favoring ideological purity, and gerrymandered districts reducing electoral competition. The polarization complicated governance, particularly during periods of divided government, while increasing the stakes of elections and institutional control. It also weakened traditional norms of political restraint and constitutional forbearance, raising concerns about democratic stability and institutional function.

Great Recession (2007-09)

Severe economic downturn following the collapse of housing market and subsequent financial crisis, representing the worst economic contraction since the Great Depression. The crisis originated in:

  • Housing bubble fueled by loose lending standards and securitization
  • Excessive leverage in financial institutions holding mortgage-backed securities
  • Regulatory gaps allowing risky practices to proliferate
  • Global financial interconnections spreading contagion across institutions and borders

The government response included unprecedented interventions in financial markets through the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP), Federal Reserve emergency lending facilities, and auto industry bailouts under Presidents Bush and Obama. The 2009 American Recovery and Reinvestment Act provided economic stimulus through infrastructure spending, tax cuts, and aid to state governments. Despite these measures, the recession caused approximately 8.7 million job losses, trillions in lost household wealth, and millions of foreclosures. The recovery proved slow and uneven, exacerbating inequality as financial assets recovered more quickly than wages and housing values. The experience shaped both political alignments and economic policy debates for the subsequent decade.

Affordable Care Act (2010)

Major healthcare reform legislation significantly expanding insurance coverage while maintaining the primarily private insurance system. Key provisions included:

  • Individual mandate requiring insurance purchase (later effectively repealed)
  • State-based insurance marketplaces with standardized plans and subsidies
  • Medicaid expansion covering more low-income Americans (made optional by Supreme Court)
  • Insurance market reforms prohibiting denial for pre-existing conditions
  • Regulations on minimum coverage requirements and medical loss ratios

The law represented the most significant healthcare legislation since Medicare and Medicaid in 1965, achieving the long-sought Democratic goal of expanding coverage while falling short of the universal single-payer system many progressives desired. It passed without Republican support and remained politically contentious, facing multiple repeal attempts and constitutional challenges. Despite implementation challenges and continuing debates about costs and market effects, the law reduced the uninsured rate from approximately 16% to 9% while establishing healthcare as a central political issue. Both its compromises and achievements reflected the continuing tensions in American healthcare between public and private approaches, federal and state authority, and competing values of access, quality, and cost control.

Recent Social Movements

Black Lives Matter

Movement against police violence and systemic racism toward Black Americans, emerging following Trayvon Martin's killing in 2012 and subsequent acquittal of George Zimmerman. The hashtag and slogan "Black Lives Matter" gained prominence during protests following police killings of Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri (2014) and many subsequent cases. The movement grew dramatically in 2020 after George Floyd's murder by Minneapolis police officer Derek Chauvin, with demonstrations in over 2,000 cities and towns representing possibly the largest protest movement in American history. BLM's demands included:

  • Police accountability and reform of use-of-force policies
  • Addressing racial disparities in criminal justice system
  • Redirecting police funding toward social services ("defund the police")
  • Broader confrontation with structural racism in housing, education, employment, and healthcare

The movement generated both significant support and backlash, becoming a focal point in American political polarization. It achieved some policy changes at local and state levels while raising awareness about racial inequities, though facing resistance to more transformative demands. BLM represented a new chapter in the long struggle for racial justice, building on civil rights movement legacy while employing new organizing strategies through social media and decentralized leadership.

LGBTQ+ Rights

Dramatic expansion of legal protections and social acceptance for lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, and queer Americans. Key developments included:

  • Decriminalization of same-sex relationships (Lawrence v. Texas, 2003)
  • Recognition of same-sex marriage (Obergefell v. Hodges, 2015)
  • Increased protections against discrimination in employment (Bostock v. Clayton County, 2020)
  • Growing transgender visibility and rights advocacy
  • Repeal of military's "Don't Ask, Don't Tell" policy (2011)

These changes represented one of the most rapid transformations in American social attitudes, with support for same-sex marriage rising from 27% in 1996 to over 70% by 2021. The movement's success reflected effective legal strategies, cultural visibility through media representation, and grassroots organizing. However, continuing challenges included violence against LGBTQ+ individuals (particularly transgender women of color), religious exemption claims in anti-discrimination laws, and debates about transgender rights in areas like sports participation and healthcare access. The movement illustrated both America's capacity for social evolution and the complex balancing of competing rights claims in a diverse society.

Me Too Movement

Campaign against sexual harassment and assault, gaining prominence in 2017 when allegations against film producer Harvey Weinstein prompted widespread sharing of personal experiences on social media with the hashtag #MeToo (phrase originally coined by activist Tarana Burke in 2006). The movement led to:

  • Public allegations against powerful figures in entertainment, politics, business, and other fields
  • Resignations and criminal charges for some accused perpetrators
  • Institutional policy changes regarding harassment reporting and investigation
  • Broader cultural conversation about consent, power dynamics, and gender relations

The movement highlighted the prevalence of sexual misconduct across industries and institutional contexts while demonstrating social media's power to rapidly transform public discourse around long-standing issues. It faced criticism regarding due process concerns, appropriate consequences for different types of misconduct, and potential chilling effects on workplace interactions. However, it succeeded in reducing stigma for survivors sharing experiences, increasing accountability for perpetrators, and establishing sexual misconduct as a serious professional and legal issue rather than normalized behavior. The movement represented a significant shift in gender politics building on earlier feminist activism while employing new technologies and cultural strategies.

Climate Activism

Growing movement addressing global climate change through political advocacy, protest, litigation, and lifestyle changes. Key developments included:

  • Youth-led initiatives like Fridays for Future and the Sunrise Movement
  • Protests against fossil fuel infrastructure such as the Keystone XL pipeline
  • Divestment campaigns pressuring institutions to withdraw investments from fossil fuel companies
  • Litigation seeking to hold governments and corporations accountable for climate impacts
  • Green New Deal proposals linking climate action with economic and social justice

The movement responded to intensifying climate impacts and scientific consensus about human-caused warming, employing both traditional environmental advocacy tactics and newer approaches emphasizing climate justice, intergenerational equity, and systemic change. It achieved variable policy success, with initiatives advancing at state and local levels despite national political polarization on climate issues. The movement represented both continuity with earlier environmental activism and evolution toward more intersectional approaches connecting climate with economic inequality, racial justice, and democratic reform. Its development reflected broader tensions in American politics between incremental and transformative change approaches, as well as competing visions of government's appropriate economic role.

Key Terms to Know

  • Supply-Side Economics: Economic theory emphasizing tax cuts and deregulation
  • Globalization: Increasing interconnection of world economies and cultures
  • North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA): 1994 agreement removing trade barriers between U.S., Canada, and Mexico
  • Berlin Wall Fall: 1989 event symbolizing the end of the Cold War
  • World Wide Web: System enabling information sharing over the Internet
  • Patriot Act: Post-9/11 legislation expanding government surveillance powers
  • Great Recession: Severe economic downturn following 2008 financial crisis
  • Affordable Care Act: 2010 healthcare reform expanding insurance coverage
  • Social Media: Online platforms enabling user-generated content sharing
  • Obergefell v. Hodges: 2015 Supreme Court case legalizing same-sex marriage nationwide
